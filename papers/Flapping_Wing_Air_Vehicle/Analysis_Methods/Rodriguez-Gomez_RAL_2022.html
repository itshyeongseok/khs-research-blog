Free as a Bird: Event-Based Dynamic Sense-and-Avoid for Ornithopter Robot Flight
Tier 1 — Top
Juan Pablo Rodriguez-Gomez, Raul Tapia, Maria del Mar Guzman Garcia, Jose Ramiro Martinez-de Dios, Anibal Ollero
IEEE Robotics and Automation Letters, Vol. 7, No. 2, pp. 5413-5420, 2022 · DOI: 10.1109/LRA.2022.3153904
Paper Summary
This paper presents the first fully onboard event-based dynamic sense-and-avoid system designed specifically for large-scale ornithopter (flapping-wing) robots, a platform category with severe payload and computational constraints that prior work had largely ignored. The system exploits the microsecond temporal resolution and high dynamic range of event cameras to detect moving obstacles via spatio-temporal event filtering, estimate their optical flow, and evaluate collision risk using robot-geometry constraints — all running event-by-event on a single lightweight onboard computer at 250 Hz. A reactive tail control law fuses a velocity-opposite evasion command with a linearized ornithopter model to produce fast, dynamics-aware deflection commands. Indoor and outdoor experiments on the GRIFFIN E-Flap robot with obstacles of varied sizes and lighting conditions demonstrate an average obstacle avoidance success rate of 89.7%, validating the practical viability of event-based perception for agile bio-inspired flight.

Introduction (click for detail)
The introduction motivates event-based sense-and-avoid for ornithopters by highlighting the unique perception challenges of flapping-wing flight: severe mechanical vibrations, strict payload limits that preclude powerful hardware, and the need for fast onboard detection of unexpected dynamic obstacles. It contrasts ornithopters with multirotors to argue that existing quadrotor-oriented event-based avoidance methods cannot be directly applied, and positions this work as the first to address the gap.
Related Works (click for detail)
The related work section surveys reactive obstacle avoidance approaches for MAVs, contrasting map-based and map-less strategies, then reviews prior event-camera applications in aerial robotics (optical flow, landing, visual servoing, intruder detection). It concludes by comparing the proposed method against three state-of-the-art event-based dynamic avoidance methods for quadrotors [8]–[10], systematically identifying why all three are unsuitable for ornithopters.
Method (click for detail)
The method section describes a three-module pipeline (Dynamic Object Motion Estimation, Collision Risk Evaluation, Tail Control) that processes events asynchronously. The Time Filter uses a velocity-adaptive threshold to isolate events from moving objects; ABMOF optical flow with corner filtering estimates their motion direction; a geometry-based collision risk evaluator determines whether an evasive maneuver is necessary; and a reactive tail controller fuses a velocity-opposite command with a linearized ornithopter model to command efficient evasion.
Experiment (click for detail)
Experiments are conducted on the GRIFFIN E-Flap ornithopter (95 cm length, 1.5 m wingspan, 510 g) equipped with a DAVIS346 event camera and Khadas VIM3 onboard computer in a 15 × 21 × 8 m OptiTrack indoor testbed and an outdoor soccer field. Evaluation covers obstacle detection accuracy (three object sizes, distances 0.5–6 m) and full sense-and-avoid success rate (indoor and outdoor, regular and dark lighting, with and without actual collision trajectories).
Discussion (click for detail)
The discussion is integrated within the experiment section and the conclusion rather than a separate section. Key points address the trade-offs of the time threshold design, the conservative geometry parameters, and the comparison of the proposed approach against prior quadrotor-based event methods in Table I, emphasizing the single-camera, single-board architecture and event-by-event latency advantages.
Conclusion (click for detail)
The conclusion reaffirms that this is the first event-based obstacle avoidance system for large-scale flapping-wing robots, summarizing the key results (89.7% average success rate, 250 Hz operation on low-power hardware) and the method's core enablers (event-by-event processing, single event camera, velocity-adaptive filtering). Two concrete future directions are stated: extension to other agile platforms, and development of a complementary map-based static obstacle avoidance module.

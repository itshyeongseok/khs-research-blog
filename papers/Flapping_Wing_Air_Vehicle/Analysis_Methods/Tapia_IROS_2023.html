A Comparison between Framed-based and Event-based Cameras for Flapping-Wing Robot Perception
Tier 1 — Top
R. Tapia, J.P. Rodriguez-Gomez, J.A. Sanchez-Diaz, F.J. Ganan, I.G. Rodriguez, J. Luna-Santamaria, J.R. Martinez-de Dios, A. Ollero
IEEE/RSJ International Conference on Intelligent Robots and Systems (IROS), 2023, pp. 3025-3032 · DOI: 10.1109/IROS55552.2023.10342500
Paper Summary
This paper presents a comprehensive experimental comparison between frame-based and event-based cameras for flapping-wing robot perception. Seven cameras including a DAVIS346 event camera are evaluated on an E-Flap ornithopter across platform requirements (weight, size, power), flight requirements (dynamic range, motion blur), and vision algorithm performance (corner detection, VIO, line detection, human detection). Results demonstrate that event cameras outperform frame cameras in power consumption, dynamic range (>120 dB), and motion blur robustness, suggesting them as the most promising sensor for ornithopter applications despite the current maturity gap in event-based software ecosystems.

Introduction (click for detail)
Flapping-wing robots face unique perception challenges due to severe vibrations, motion blur, and strict payload constraints. While cameras are the primary sensor choice, there is no consensus on whether frame-based or event-based cameras are more suitable, motivating this systematic experimental comparison.
Related Works (click for detail)
Prior ornithopter perception systems relied on external sensors or basic onboard cameras, with recent works exploring event cameras. While theoretical comparisons exist between frame and event cameras, no systematic experimental comparison had been conducted specifically for flapping-wing robots.
Method (click for detail)
Seven cameras spanning event-based, monocular, and stereo configurations were evaluated using three test benches emulating flapping conditions and actual flight experiments aboard the E-Flap ornithopter. The comparison framework addresses platform requirements, flight requirements, and application-dependent performance.
Experiment (click for detail)
Event cameras demonstrated lower power consumption, superior dynamic range, and higher motion blur robustness compared to frame cameras. In flight data experiments, APS and DVS achieved comparable performance, with event-based single-event representations providing the fastest input rates.
Discussion (click for detail)
The results suggest event cameras as the most suitable sensors for ornithopters due to superior dynamic range, motion blur robustness, and competitive algorithm performance. However, the frame-based software ecosystem is considerably more mature, and onboard processing bottlenecks remain an open challenge.
Conclusion (click for detail)
The paper establishes event cameras as the most promising vision sensor for ornithopter perception through systematic experimental evaluation. While APS and DVS achieved similar algorithm performance, event cameras' inherent advantages in dynamic range, motion blur robustness, and power consumption make them the preferred choice.

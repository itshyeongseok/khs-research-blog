Flapping-wing robot achieves bird-style self-takeoff by adopting reconfigurable mechanisms
Tier 1 — Top
Ang Chen, Bifeng Song, Kang Liu, Zhihe Wang, Dong Xue, Hongduo Qi
Science Advances, Vol. 11, No. 36, eadx0465 (2025) · DOI: 10.1126/sciadv.adx0465
Paper Summary
This paper presents RoboFalcon2.0, a medium-bird-scale flapping-wing robot capable of bird-style self-takeoff through a bioinspired flap-sweep-fold (FSF) wing motion enabled by novel reconfigurable mechanisms. The robot's conical rocker mechanism (CRM) combined with a folding decoupler and sweeping decoupler couples wing flapping, sweeping, and folding within a single wingbeat cycle, replicating the ventral-anterior downstroke with aerodynamically inactive tucked upstroke characteristic of flying vertebrates at low airspeeds. Wind tunnel experiments and CFD simulations demonstrate that increasing sweeping amplitude enhances both lift and nose-up pitching moment by strengthening the leading-edge vortex (LEV) and shifting the center of pressure (CP) forward, enabling an underactuation strategy where one actuator concurrently controls two degrees of freedom. Real-world flight tests on a prototype (1.2 m wingspan, 800 g) confirm controlled bird-style self-takeoff, validating the reconfigurable sweeping amplitude as an effective pitch-control strategy for the low-airspeed takeoff phase.

Introduction (click for detail)
The introduction motivates the work by documenting the slow-flight wingbeat kinematics of flying vertebrates — specifically the asymmetrical hovering pattern featuring ventral-anterior downstrokes and aerodynamically inactive folded upstrokes — and contrasting it with the single-DOF wing kinematics universally adopted by existing flapping-wing robots. It argues that the full FSF (flap-sweep-fold) coupling characteristic of birds and bats during takeoff, landing, and hovering has never been implemented on a self-takeoff-capable robot, and that a reconfigurable mechanism approach can resolve the actuator weight-power-bandwidth contradiction inherent in multi-DOF wing drive systems.
Related Works (click for detail)
Related work encompasses three threads: (1) biologically documented bird and bat wingbeat kinematics studies that established the aerodynamic significance of inclined stroke planes and anterior wing protracting at low airspeeds; (2) previous robotic implementations of wing sweeping and folding features in drones and flapping-wing robots that stopped short of slow-flight or self-takeoff applications; and (3) the authors' own prior CRM-based RoboFalcon platform capable of morphing-coupled cruise flight but limited to cruise-flight kinematics. The gap identified is the absence of any robot implementing ventral-anterior FSF wingbeat for self-takeoff.
Method (click for detail)
The method section presents the mechanical design of RoboFalcon2.0 (1.2 m wingspan, 800 g, peregrine-falcon scale) centered on two new reconfigurable decoupler mechanisms — the folding decoupler and the sweeping decoupler — that, in combination with the previously developed CRM, convert the rotation of a single BLDC motor into fully coupled yet independently adjustable FSF wing motion. Aerodynamic characterization uses wind tunnel force measurements and CFD (SU2 solver, incompressible N-S equations) across three representative flight states, while a quasi-steady strip-theory aerodynamic model drives real-time MuJoCo dynamics simulation for takeoff dynamics analysis.
Experiment (click for detail)
Experiments proceed in three phases: wind tunnel characterization of FSF aerodynamic forces across flight states and sweeping amplitudes, CFD flow visualization to explain the physical mechanisms, and real-world takeoff flight tests on the RoboFalcon2.0 prototype tethered to a ceiling-mounted string in a 15 m hangar. Results confirm that larger sweeping amplitude simultaneously enhances lift (especially at low airspeed) and head-up pitching moment, that this originates from a stronger leading-edge vortex and forward-shifted center of pressure, and that real-world bird-style self-takeoff is achievable with appropriate CG configuration.
Discussion (click for detail)
The discussion contextualizes RoboFalcon2.0 as the first robotic platform to perform bird-style self-takeoff via FSF wing motion driven by reconfigurable mechanisms, highlighting the underactuation strategy coupling lift and pitching-moment control through sweeping amplitude as a key functional advantage that reduces actuator count and simplifies attitude control. The section honestly acknowledges the boundary of the approach — effective only in the low-airspeed takeoff phase — and proposes multiple concrete future hardware pathways to extend the flight envelope to cruise and perching landing.
Conclusion (click for detail)
The conclusion affirms that RoboFalcon2.0 successfully demonstrates bird-style self-takeoff through reconfigurable FSF wing motion, validated by wind tunnel aerodynamics, CFD flow visualization, MuJoCo dynamics simulation, and real-world flight tests. It positions the work as an advance in avian-inspired robotics that bridges the kinematic gap between existing single-DOF flapping robots and the vertebrate slow-flight wing kinematics, while acknowledging that full-envelope flight remains an open challenge.

A Novel Actuation Strategy for an Agile Bioinspired FWAV Performing a Morphing-Coupled Wingbeat Pattern
Tier 1 — Top
Ang Chen, Bifeng Song, Zhihe Wang, Dong Xue, Kang Liu
IEEE Transactions on Robotics, Vol. 39, No. 1, pp. 452-469, 2023 · DOI: 10.1109/TRO.2022.3189812
Paper Summary
This paper presents RoboFalcon, a bioinspired flapping-wing aerial vehicle (FWAV) that achieves a morphing-coupled wingbeat pattern — extending wings during the downstroke and tucking them during the upstroke — by means of a novel conical rocker mechanism (CRM). A morphing decoupler enables independent servo-driven wing morphing during maneuvers, allowing bilateral asymmetric downstrokes that generate rolling moments correlated with flapping frequency. Wind tunnel experiments with four wrist mounting angles demonstrate that the wrist angle tunes the angle of attack, lift/thrust balance, and power consumption of the equilibrium flight state, while the morphing-coupled pattern yields higher lift efficiency than conventional wingbeat. Tethered and free-flight tests confirm that RoboFalcon completes a 90-degree rolling maneuver in as few as 2.5 wingbeat cycles, and a system-identification-based lateral linear model shows close agreement with measured flight data under simple PID control.

Introduction (click for detail)
Flying vertebrates uniquely couple wing morphing with wingbeat — extending wings in the downstroke and tucking them in the upstroke — to reduce drag and enhance agility, but no prior flyable bionic platform had reproduced this pattern. The paper motivates the design of RoboFalcon, a FWAV that achieves the morphing-coupled wingbeat through a novel mechanism, performs bilateral asymmetric downstrokes for high rolling agility, and is validated through wind tunnel and free-flight experiments.
Related Works (click for detail)
Prior bioinspired FWAV research has explored feathered and membranous morphing wings as well as various mechanisms for coupling or decoupling morphing from flapping, but the morphing-coupled wingbeat pattern has remained limited to wind tunnel and simulation studies. The paper situates RoboFalcon within this landscape, identifying the gap in flyable platforms that achieve biologically faithful coupled kinematics.
Method (click for detail)
RoboFalcon's design centers on two novel mechanisms: the conical rocker mechanism (CRM) that mechanically couples wing flapping and morphing at the same frequency, and a morphing decoupler consisting of three interlocking sliders that allows the servo to take over wing morphing during the downstroke for independent bilateral control. The bat-style membranous wing skeleton includes a tilted wrist joint mounting angle that mimics wrist supination/pronation to passively tune the hand-wing twist.
Experiment (click for detail)
Experiments are organized in three parts: wind tunnel aerodynamic characterization of four wrist mounting angles and comparison of morphing-coupled versus conventional wingbeat, tethered rolling maneuver tests for open-loop agility assessment, and outdoor free-flight tests with onboard data logging. The results quantify the influence of wrist angle on equilibrium flight state, confirm higher lift efficiency of the morphing-coupled wingbeat, and demonstrate exceptional rolling agility.
Discussion (click for detail)
The discussion evaluates RoboFalcon's contributions in terms of bionic fidelity, aerodynamic insight, and agility, while acknowledging the limitations imposed by the membrane wing and the scope boundary of the article. The authors situate the platform as a research tool for studying bird/bat flight mechanics and as a proof-of-concept for the morphing-coupled actuation strategy.
Conclusion (click for detail)
RoboFalcon demonstrates, for the first time on a flyable platform, the morphing-coupled wingbeat pattern analogous to the level flight kinematics of birds, enabled by the conical rocker mechanism and the morphing decoupler. The integrated experimental validation — wind tunnel characterization, tethered maneuver tests, and outdoor free flight with system identification — establishes both the aerodynamic insight and the agility performance of the proposed actuation strategy.

How ornithopters can perch autonomously on a branch
Tier 1 — Top
Raphael Zufferey, Jesus Tormo-Barbero, Daniel Feliu-Talegon, Saeed Rafee Nekoo, Jose Angel Acosta, Anibal Ollero
Nature Communications, Vol. 13, 7713, 2022 · DOI: 10.1038/s41467-022-35356-5
Paper Summary
This paper presents the first demonstrated method for a large flapping-wing robot (ornithopter) to autonomously perch on a branch, addressing a long-standing gap in bio-inspired aerial robotics. The proposed three-component method jointly operates a pitch-yaw-altitude flight controller, an optical close-range correction system using a line-scan sensor on an active leg, and a bistable claw appendage that mechanically grasps a branch within 25 milliseconds on contact. The system is validated on a 700 g, 150 cm wingspan robot (the P-Flap) in an indoor motion-capture arena, achieving 6 successful branch perchings out of 9 attempts at forward speeds of 2.5–3 m/s. Key design challenges overcome include impact resistance, tolerating altitude oscillations from the flapping gait, misalignment compensation via the active leg, and enabling re-opening of the claw without continuous power draw. This work opens the door to energy-saving long-duration missions, wildlife observation, and physical interaction tasks previously unattainable with large flapping-wing platforms.

Introduction (click for detail)
The introduction motivates branch perching for flapping-wing robots by highlighting their unique safety and ecological advantages for wildlife monitoring, physical interaction, and long-duration missions enabled by energy conservation on a branch. It surveys the landscape of perching solutions for fixed-wing and multirotor UAVs, identifies the gap that no large ornithopter had ever achieved branch perching, and frames the scaling physics that make this problem hard: impact kinetic energy scales as $L^3 v^2$ while appendage strength scales only as $L^2$, forcing large robots to land slowly yet above a minimum speed needed to sustain flight.
Related Works (click for detail)
The related work section reviews the broad landscape of UAV perching solutions and contextualizes why each approach is insufficient for large forward-flying ornithopters. It highlights the progression from passive attachment mechanisms on multirotors to recent catapult-launched fixed-wing perching, and notes the complete absence of branch perching results for large flapping-wing robots despite growing interest in aerial physical interaction.
Method (click for detail)
The method section details the three interlocking subsystems that together enable autonomous branch perching: the bistable claw appendage with an integrated re-opening mechanism, the active leg with line-scan close-range correction, and the triple-loop pitch-yaw-altitude flight controller, all hosted on a custom structural PCB within the P-Flap robot. A four-step experimental development process (launcher, flight-only, soft-branch, full perching) is proposed to safely validate and tune each subsystem iteratively before integration.
Experiment (click for detail)
Experiments follow the four-step development process: launcher-only claw impact tests establish robustness up to 4 m/s; flight-only tests tune the pitch-yaw-altitude controller; soft-branch tests validate the active leg close-range correction; and full perching on a real wooden branch achieves 6 successes out of 9 attempts at 2.5–3 m/s, replicated with a second robot without controller modification.
Discussion (click for detail)
The discussion contextualizes the demonstration as the first large-scale autonomous branch perching by any flapping-wing robot, emphasizes the role of each subsystem in achieving it, and honestly identifies the controlled conditions that currently bound the result. It then outlines a clear roadmap from indoor controlled demonstration toward outdoor autonomous perching, claw material improvements, and future take-off from the branch.
Conclusion (click for detail)
The conclusion summarizes the paper's main contribution as the first demonstration of autonomous branch perching by a large flapping-wing robot, achieved through the simultaneous operation of a flight controller, active leg with close-range correction, and bistable claw, and positions this result as a milestone enabling a broad class of future long-duration and interaction missions for ornithopters.
